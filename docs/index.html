How to use Going Yard

A daily board that ranks hitters on how likely they are to go yard, using free Statcast + MLB data. Everything updates itself through the day — no input needed. It's a research tool, not betting advice.

The board

Heat (0–100) ranks each hitter on six power signals over the last 2 weeks — Pull-air%, Barrel%, ISO, Exit Velo, Ideal Attack Angle%, SLG — then nudges for the opposing pitcher's vulnerability. Higher = better HR spot. The chip runs blue (cold) → orange (elite).

The four cells (Pull / EV / Brl / IAA) show those signals at a glance — orange means it cleared the "good" mark, blue means below. Tap any column header (HEAT, PULL, EV, BRL, IAA) to re-sort the whole slate by that stat — that's how you find a low-Heat guy who's elite in one area.

Tap any row to open full detail: recent/season/career for every signal, the trend windows, the opposing arm, bullpen, pitch matchup, and luck.

Badges & arrows

R / L / S (orange/blue/purple) = bats right, left, or switch. Tier (LOADED / STRONG / SOLID / LEAN / THIN) = how many of the six signals cleared "good" — a confirmation read independent of Heat. ▲ / ▼ = trending up or down in power form (recent vs baseline), with % change. proj = lineup not posted yet, using last game's order as a placeholder. #1–#9 = batting-order spot.

Filters & sorting

Heat / Trend / Name / Time sort the list. 40+/55+/70+ cut the long tail by Heat floor. All games jumps to one matchup. Bats R/L/S shows only that handedness. Hide small samples drops hitters with under ~25 PA in the window (their numbers are noise).

Top Plays & Stacks

Top Plays (panel up top) = the strongest non-thin hitters not facing a sharp arm, each with a one-line "why" — your fast scan. Stacks (view toggle) groups hot hitters by the vulnerable arm they share — built for grand-slam parlays / same-game pairing.

The pitcher

Arms Today ranks starters by how shellable they are. Form labels: SHELLABLE (bad & getting worse — prime), STEADY-BAD (consistently hittable — still a target), SLIPPING (was fine, now cracking), BAD-IMPROVING / BOUNCING-BACK (downgrade — numbers improving), DEALING (avoid). Platoon shows whether the arm gets hit harder by RHB or LHB, and the score vs your hitter's hand. Opposing bullpen shows the pen's HR-vulnerability and its split vs your hitter's hand.

Pitch matchup & luck (context only)

vs Pitch Mix toggle (top controls): re-ranks the board by each hitter's EV/launch-angle/whiff weighted to the opposing starter's pitch mix over the last 2 weeks — a guy who crushes fastballs rises against a fastball-heavy arm. Toggle off to return to standard Heat instantly. Barrel% stays overall. Each card shows the mix-adjusted EV and how Heat shifts.

Pitch matchup = the hitter's barrel% by pitch family (Fastball/Breaking/Offspeed) crossed with the arm's usage. Contact luck = expected (xwOBAcon) vs actual; positive gap = hitting it well but unlucky ("due"), negative = "regression risk." Starter HRs by hand = actual HRs the starter allowed to RHB vs LHB, over a 2-year sample and this year. None of these affect Heat unless the mix toggle is on.

Tracker

Grades the model against reality each morning. Model vs baselines is the one that matters: does Heat's top 10 homer more than just sorting by ISO or barrel%? If yes, the model is genuinely finding HR hitters better than the simple stats. The rest shows HR rate by Heat tier, by arm form, top-N success, which signals correlate to HRs, and a daily log. It needs a few graded days to mean anything.

How it updates

The board rebuilds hourly through the day as lineups post; the grader runs each morning after games are final. Park factor is shown for context but isn't baked into Heat — the data and matchup drive the ranking. All of it is tunable, and none of it is betting advice.
