How to use Going Yard

A daily board that ranks hitters on how likely they are to go yard, using free Statcast + MLB data. Everything updates itself through the day — no input needed. It's a research tool, not betting advice.

Start here — you don't need to know baseball

  1. Open the Board. It's every hitter playing today, ranked. The big number is Heat — how hard and how well this guy has been hitting the ball for the last two weeks. Higher is better.
  2. Go to the Bets tab → Find Edge. This is the one that matters.
  3. Each hitter shows a line like "bet only if +550 or longer." Open your sportsbook, find that hitter's home run price, and compare.
  4. If the book's number is bigger than the one shown (+600 beats +550), type it in. The app tells you if it's a good bet.
  5. If the book's number is smaller (+400 is worse than +550), skip it — even if he's the hottest hitter on the board.

That last point is the whole thing. A great hitter at a bad price loses money. A mediocre hitter at a great price makes money. The app exists to tell you which is which — the Heat number finds the hitters, the price decides the bet.

Most days, the honest answer is "no bet." Books price home runs pretty well. Passing is a decision, not a failure.

The board

Heat (0–100) ranks each hitter on six power signals over the last 2 weeks — Pull-air%, Barrel%, ISO, Exit Velo, Ideal Attack Angle%, SLG — then nudges for the opposing pitcher's vulnerability. Higher = better HR spot. The chip runs blue (cold) → orange (elite).

The four cells (Pull / EV / Brl / IAA) show those signals at a glance — orange means it cleared the "good" mark, blue means below. Tap any column header (HEAT, PULL, EV, BRL, IAA) to re-sort the whole slate by that stat — that's how you find a low-Heat guy who's elite in one area.

Tap any row to open full detail: recent/season/career for every signal, the trend windows, the opposing arm, bullpen, pitch matchup, and luck.

Confirmation tier — LOADED / STRONG / SOLID / LEAN / THIN

The pill next to a hitter's name isn't about sample size — it counts how many of the six signals cleared their "good" anchor. LOADED = 5–6 cleared, STRONG = 4, SOLID = 3, LEAN = 2, THIN = 0–1.

Heat tells you how hot; the tier tells you how many independent things agree. A 68 Heat that's LOADED is a hitter where nearly everything is firing. A 68 Heat that's LEAN is one or two signals carrying the whole score — same number, more fragile. Use it as a breadth check before you commit.

Thin-data warning. A signal can be missing rather than just low — a just-called-up or returning-from-injury hitter may not have data for every metric yet. When fewer than all six have real numbers, the score fills the blanks with a cautious "poor" baseline, and the card shows a ⚠ thin-data warning. Treat that Heat as softer than it looks — it's resting partly on absence of evidence. This is why two hitters at the same Heat can be very different bets.

SMASH bar

When a hitter clears a high bar on multiple fronts at once — strong heat, several confirming reasons, a genuinely vulnerable spot — he gets flagged SMASH and appears in the morning briefing. It's deliberately rare (top few per slate). Treat it as "the model is unusually confident," not a guarantee. Still accumulating sample; check the Tracker before sizing anything off it.

Robbed Board & Milestone Watch

Robbed flags hitters who hit a ball in the last 3 days that would have left most parks — a 400+ ft drive that died at the wall or got caught. It's a "the process was right, the result wasn't" signal. The ROBBED ×N badge shows how many times. Tap the card for the spray chart.

Milestone Watch surfaces hitters sitting near a round-number career HR mark (100, 150, 200…) or within a few HRs of passing a franchise leader. Not a model signal — a context signal. Some bettors like the narrative spot; the model doesn't care either way.

My List

Star any hitter to add him to My List, then tap the ★ My List chip to filter the board to only those hitters. The list auto-clears when the slate date changes — yesterday's picks never bleed into today. A Clear button appears next to the chip whenever the list has anything in it, if you want to wipe it early.

Badges

R / L / S (orange/blue/purple) = a hitter bats right, left, or switch; pitchers show RHP / LHP in the same colors. The trait badges flag why a hitter is interesting — matchup first: WEAK ARM (facing a shellable starter), PLATOON (strong HR history vs this arm's hand), PITCH EDGE (barrels the pitch this arm throws most), POWER (top-end exit velo, 112+ mph).

Then one form read per hitter — a simple spectrum so you never see a contradiction: DUE (hitting it hard but unlucky — results lag his contact), WARMING (contact quality trending up), HOT (elite contact and results match — genuinely hot, earned), MAY COOL (results have run well above his contact and may regress). ▲ / ▼ = power-form trend with % change. proj = lineup not posted, using last game's order. #1–#9 = batting-order spot.

Filters & sorting

Heat / Trend / BP / Bomb / Park / Name / Time sort the list — BP ranks by how vulnerable the opposing bullpen is to each hitter's hand, Bomb ranks by raw long-ball ceiling (season max exit velo nudged by recent form; the "NNN EV" chip shows the ceiling), and Park ranks by today's park & weather HR boost (the chip shows the % swing plus wind).

Park & weather model

A separate, physics-based read — its own lens that never touches the Heat score. It flies each hitter's actual batted balls (exit velocity, launch angle, spray direction) through the real wall distances and heights of today's park, under today's temperature and wind, and counts how many clear the fence versus a neutral park. That gives a per-hitter boost, not a flat park factor: a dead-pull hitter gets more from a short porch on his side, and wind/heat scale with how often he already lives near the wall. Tap any row for the full read — expected HR per 100 batted balls here vs neutral, plus the conditions used. Two honest limits, shared by every public version of this (Ballpark Pal included): batted-ball backspin is estimated from launch conditions, not measured, and closed retractable roofs can't be detected, so treat it as a strong secondary signal — and for the final per-hitter precision, Ballpark Pal is still the authority.

The 30-park read. On the same expanded card, a 30-PARK block takes the hitter's recent deep contact and flies it through all 30 MLB parks' walls under neutral air — pure geometry, no weather. It tells you how homer-friendly his contact is park to park: a guy whose clearing balls average 28 of 30 parks is "out almost anywhere," while one averaging 8-12 "needs a friendly park." It also shows how many of his deep balls clear tonight's park specifically, and how many are park-dependent (out in some parks but not others — the ones where the venue decides his night). Same principle as the boost above, but framed as fit rather than a percentage — useful for spotting a hitter whose power plays up or down badly in tonight's building.

In ⚙ Filters: Games and Teams narrow to specific matchups or clubs, Heat floor (40/55/70+) cuts the long tail, Opposing starter drops strong SP so you only see hitters facing a vulnerable one, Bats shows one handedness, and Traits lets you tap any badge to show only hitters carrying it (tap several to stack them). Every filter combines, so you can stack (say) a vulnerable starter + weak bullpen sort + a trait + a team at once. Hide small samples drops hitters under ~25 PA in the window or whose score leans on missing signal data (fewer than 4 of the 6 signals have numbers).

Top Plays & Stacks

Top Plays (panel up top) = the strongest non-thin hitters not facing a sharp arm, each with a one-line "why" — your fast scan. Stacks (view toggle) groups hot hitters by the vulnerable arm they share — built for grand-slam parlays / same-game pairing.

The pitcher

Arms Today ranks starters by how shellable they are. Form labels: SHELLABLE (bad & getting worse — prime), STEADY-BAD (consistently hittable — still a target), SLIPPING (was fine, now cracking), BAD-IMPROVING / BOUNCING-BACK (downgrade — numbers improving), DEALING (avoid). Arm badges spell out the specific vulnerability behind the label: HR PRONE (gives up bombs), BARRELED (barreled ≥10%), LOUD (hard contact/high EV allowed), PULL-AIR (pulled fly balls allowed), WEAK vs R/L (which hand's taken him deep over 2yr — target those hitters), VELO ↓ (velo trending down), SLIPPING (recent worse than season), TOUGH (low vulnerability — avoid). Platoon shows whether the arm gets hit harder by RHB or LHB, and the score vs your hitter's hand. Opposing bullpen shows the pen's HR-vulnerability and its split vs your hitter's hand.

Tap any arm to expand it — you'll see every batter facing him tonight, ranked by Heat, plus a link to filter the board to just those bats. The Sort toggle at the top of the panel switches between "By vulnerability" and "Most hittable." The expanded view also shows a HRs allowed by hand table with two windows: YTD (this season only) and 2szn (this season + last, regular season only). Reference tools like PropFinder default to season-only, so if a number ever looks off, check which window you're reading — they're both there on purpose.

Pitch matchup & luck (context only)

vs Pitch Mix toggle (top controls): re-ranks the board by each hitter's EV/launch-angle/whiff weighted to the opposing starter's pitch mix over the last 2 weeks — a guy who crushes fastballs rises against a fastball-heavy arm. Toggle off to return to standard Heat instantly. Barrel% stays overall. Each card shows the mix-adjusted EV and how Heat shifts.

Pitch matchup = the hitter's barrel% by pitch family (Fastball/Breaking/Offspeed) crossed with the arm's usage. Contact luck = expected (xwOBAcon) vs actual; a positive gap means he's hitting it well but unlucky (DUE). Overperforming only flags MAY COOL when results run far above his contact; if the underlying contact is itself elite he reads HOT — earned, not lucky. Starter HRs by hand = actual HRs the starter allowed to RHB vs LHB, over a 2-year sample and this year. None of these affect Heat unless the mix toggle is on.

Live HR Tracker

A panel on the board that polls MLB's live feed every 90 seconds while games are on. Every home run that lands shows up with the hitter's full board identity from this morning — his Heat chip, confirmation tier, the exact badges he carried, his board rank, all three prop scores, plus distance and RBI count. A ★ means he was on your list. Hitters who homer but weren't on the board show as "off board."

The point isn't the scores — it's the cross-reference. Over a night you can see whether the model's picks are the ones cashing, in real time, rather than waiting for the grader next morning. The dot pulses red while games are live; polling pauses when you close the tab and stops entirely once every game is Final.

Other Props tab

Four prop models that run completely separate from the HR heat model — different signals, different scores, their own tracker and backtest. HR heat is never touched by any of them.

Hits — ranks hitters for 1+ / 2+ hits using xBA, hard-hit%, line-drive%, plate discipline (BB% minus K%), contact%, and the opposing arm's hittability. HRR — hits + runs + RBIs, which is hit skill plus a lineup-spot multiplier (top of the order gets more PAs and more RBI chances) plus an HR-upside boost, since one swing can produce all three. Pitcher Ks — ranks starters for their K-total prop using their own K% and SwStr% blended over 2 weeks, weighted against the opposing lineup's K vulnerability. Each card shows an estimated K total and target line ("est. 7.8 Ks · line target O7.5") so you can compare directly against the book before opening the odds screen. Openers get an automatic downgrade — they can't throw enough innings to clear a normal line.

K Under — sorted low to high on purpose. The lowest K-prone scores are the best "UNDER 0.5 strikeouts" bets: contact hitters facing low-whiff arms. Tags run STRONG UNDER (under 30) → LEAN UNDER → NEUTRAL → FADE (60+, the K-prone guys you'd avoid).

All four use the same 2-week trailing window as the HR model, with the same thin-sample discount on both the batter and pitcher side.

Tracker

Grades every model against reality each morning. Model vs baselines is the one that matters: does Heat's top 10 homer more than just sorting by ISO or barrel%? If yes, the model is genuinely finding HR hitters better than the simple stats. The rest shows HR rate by Heat tier, by arm form, top-N success, which signals correlate to HRs, and a daily log.

Parlay performance shows how the auto-generated parlays actually converted — the leg hit rate is the honest number; if it isn't beating the base rate, that strategy isn't earning. Props performance does the same for hits, 2+ hits, HRR, pitcher Ks, and K Under, each with its own base rate derived from your own data (never a hard-coded league average).

Season backtest replays the whole season with a strict no-leakage contract — every score for a given day is computed only from data before that day, and a poison check corrupts future rows to prove the scores don't move. It covers HR heat plus all the props models, so you can see whether a model has a real edge before you've spent a month collecting live data.

Calibration — why the backtest is the most important number in the app. As it replays, it records the actual HR rate at each Heat band across ~24,000 hitter-days. That real curve — not a formula — is what powers the Edge Finder's probabilities. When you see "these numbers are calibrated" on the Bets tab, this is what it means. A clean result looks like HR rate climbing steadily with Heat (roughly 5% at the bottom to 16%+ up top); the current backtest shows exactly that, which is the proof your Heat model genuinely predicts homers rather than just ranking hitters. Re-run the backtest after any change to the scoring model (the app tags each with a model version and warns on the Bets tab if the loaded calibration is stale) so the Edge Finder keeps pricing off numbers that match how Heat is currently computed.

Rows marked thin and dimmed mean the sample is too small to trust yet (under 30 for hitters, 20 for pitchers). The number is real math, but it could easily be noise. Wait for it to fill in.

How to Bet

The HOW TO BET button in the header opens a guide that rewrites itself from your own tracker data. It sorts every signal into buckets — use (big enough sample, real lift), tiebreak (real sample, thin edge), watch (interesting but not enough data yet), fade (actually negative), ignore (too thin to say). As your sample grows the guide changes. It is not static advice; it's whatever your data currently supports.

Bets — Find Edge

The tab that turns the model into a decision. Every hitter shows the model's probability and the worst price you can take and still break even. Type in what your book is posting and it computes the expected value instantly, then sorts everything by it.

Why this exists: beating the base rate is not the same as making money. A hitter the model likes at 17% needs +488 or longer to break even. Books post home run props at +300 to +450. So a 17% hitter at +400 is a losing bet — you'd be paying 20 cents for something worth 17. Meanwhile a 12.5% hitter at +800 is profitable, because the book is paying you for 11% when the true odds are 12.5%.

That inversion is the entire game, and it's why the hottest hitter on the board is frequently the worst bet on the board. Prices you enter clear automatically each new slate.

The caveat that matters: a positive EV number is only as trustworthy as the model's calibration. Until the Tracker shows that a stated 17% actually converts near 17%, these are candidates, not green lights.

Bets — My Bets

Your private bet log — stored only on this device, nothing leaves your phone. Log each bet, pick the player(s) from today's board (their badges and Heat are captured automatically), then after games mark each as HR ✓ or no HR and set the result. Over time it shows your record, net, and ROI — plus the payoff feature: HR rate by badge, so you learn which badges actually convert for you (does HOT homer more than MAY COOL?) and which are noise.

Auto-grading. While games are live, the app watches the home run feed and fills in your legs for you — when a hitter you bet goes deep, his leg flips to HR ✓ with a small auto tag so you know it came from the feed, not your own tap. You can always override it. Players who get scratched from the lineup are flagged SCRATCHED — at most books an HR prop on a scratch is voided and refunded, so that leg probably doesn't count.

Closing Line Value (CLV) — the number that actually matters. After a line closes, type the closing price into any logged bet. CLV measures whether you got a better price than where the market ended up. If you took +500 and it closed +400, you beat the close — that's positive CLV, and it's the single most reliable sign your edge is real. Here's why it matters more than wins: you can lose a bet you were right to make, and win a bet you were wrong to make — over a small sample, win/loss is mostly luck. But consistently beating the closing line can't be luck. If your CLV is positive across a few dozen bets, the profits will follow even if your early record looks rough. If it's negative, the market is telling you your reads are off, no matter how many you happen to win. Watch this number more than your record.

Freshness

The dot by the title shows build health: green = fresh, amber = a few hours old, red = likely stale or showing an older slate. The board builds at 6:30am, 10am, 12pm, 3pm, 5pm and 7pm ET; if one is missed or you're viewing yesterday's slate, a banner warns you before you bet.

How it updates

The board rebuilds through the day (6:30am, 10am, 12pm, 3pm, 5pm, 7pm ET) as lineups post; the grader runs each morning after games are final. Expanded cards show a heat trajectory sparkline — the hitter's last 10 days of Heat, with dashed lines at the 55 and 70 tier marks — so you can see the shape of a trend, not just an up/down arrow. Park factor is shown for context but isn't baked into Heat — the data and matchup drive the ranking.

Every model here is honest about what it doesn't know. Thin samples get flagged, new models get labeled as unvalidated until the tracker and backtest say otherwise, and none of it is betting advice.
